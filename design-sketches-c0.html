Why it works: The vertical wall is a physical metaphor for an information barrier — teenagers get this immediately. Colors on the left are vivid; the right is deliberately flat and gray. The 1:1 row alignment makes clear that these are the same four cars — just seen differently. The "best guess" box shows that the buyer has to rely on averages.

Potential issue: Two-column layout may read as "two separate groups of cars" rather than "the same cars from two perspectives." This could be resolved by adding a subtle connector arrow or matching row heights exactly.

Why it works: The physical dashed line cutting each card in half is a powerful metaphor — same object, two realities. The color/gray contrast is immediate. Works well in small-group or laptop viewing where you're close to the screen.

Potential issue: Each card is narrow (~160px wide at full size). The buyer's half has a lot squeezed in. On projection at 15+ feet the bottom half labels ("price unknown") may be unreadable. The four-across layout also means this fails on a narrow screen.
Concept C
Thought Bubbles
Four seller-car pairs at the top, each with a thought bubble showing what they privately know. A hazard-stripe barrier in the middle. One buyer at the bottom who can only see gray cars and guess an average. Most narrative of the three — tells a story.

Why it works: Thought bubbles are a universally understood visual language. The hazard-stripe barrier is visceral. Most importantly, the layout tells the story spatially — information flows down from sellers, hits a wall, and what reaches the buyer is degraded. Matches how Rick will narrate it.

Potential issue: Tallest of the three designs — requires significant vertical space. The seller figures (emoji people) are optional; they personalize the story but add height. The buyer section might need to be condensed for mobile/narrow screens.
