Does it illustrate the point?
Yes — the fading mechanic (exited sellers go to 18% opacity) is conceptually correct and the progressive disappearance of quality tiers is the core idea. The badge ("⭐", "✓", "⚠", "🍋") adds a quick-read quality signal.
Issue 1 — Color coding fights intuition
Problem: Mint = blue, Good = green, Fair = orange, Lemon = yellow. Blue doesn't mean "best" to most people — gold/amber does. And yellow for "lemon" is visually correct (lemon = yellow), but blue for premium feels arbitrary, not earned. A 16-year-old will have to consciously decode this instead of reading it at a glance.
Fix: Mint → gold/amber (#c9a227 — you already have this as your accent color!), Good → green (keep), Fair → orange (keep), Lemon → red. This maps to a stop-light / traffic-light mental model that is instantly readable.
Issue 2 — 9px value labels are invisible on projection
Problem: The per-token price labels (e.g., "$20K", "$14K") are rendered at font-size: 9px. On a 1080p projector at 15 feet, these are physically smaller than 2mm. Anyone past the third row cannot read them. These labels are doing important conceptual work — they show why the $20K seller refuses the $11K offer.
Fix: Move the value label inside or directly below the vehicle body at ≥13px. Or display the tier value once per row in the left-side label column instead of repeating it on every token.
Issue 3 — "?" gray tokens are indistinct
Problem: In "No Info" and "Symmetric" modes, all tokens render as the same flat gray (#9ca3af) with a "?" badge. On a projected screen they look like a uniform gray blob — no tier structure is visible. The point (everyone sees the same thing) is correct, but visually there's nothing to distinguish 10 identical gray cars from one another.
Fix: Keep the gray bodies but give each tier row a slightly different gray shade, or use a subtle outline in the eventual tier color (so the structure is hinted at, even when hidden). This lets the reveal feel more dramatic when the asymmetric colors appear.

Does it illustrate the point?
Yes — the faded rows (exited sellers) are the strongest element here. Watching the table shrink step by step makes the math undeniable. The buyer's offer row at the bottom lands the "this is what the rational buyer would pay" point cleanly.
Issue — Too dense for a projected classroom screen
Problem: Four columns, six rows, 12px body text, 3px row padding. This table is sized for reading at arm's length on a laptop, not for projecting in a classroom. The "Total" column adds precision but may just add clutter — students already grasp "$8K × 3 = $24K" if you say it aloud.
Fix: Increase row padding to ≥10px and bump body font to 14–15px. Consider dropping the "Total" column to simplify to Quality / Count / Value. The average row (the key number) should be visually larger than the data rows — not just bold.

Does it illustrate the point?
Partly. The three-row format is clean and the color-coded cost figures (green / blue / red) create a gradient of severity. But the visual doesn't communicate the magnitude of the jump — $40 → $100K is 2,500× more expensive, yet all three rows are the same height and visual weight. The most important data point in the panel (the scale of the disparity) is essentially hidden.
The core problem: This is the "aha" moment of the entire talk — that information costs scale with stakes. But visually, a $40 fix and a $10M/yr fix look identical on screen. A teenager looking at this panel would need to calculate the difference mentally rather than feel it.
Fix: Add a simple horizontal bar behind each cost figure, scaled logarithmically. Even a rough bar (e.g., 4px for $40, 12px for Free-but-effortful, 80px for $10M+) makes the scale visible at a glance. Or use dramatically different font sizes for the cost figures — 14px for $40, 22px for $100K–$10M+.

✅ The Fix: Carfax~$40 per report

In 1984, R.L. Polk created a vehicle history database. For about $40, any buyer can pull a full report: accidents, ownership history, mileage, service records. Sellers of good cars can now prove it.

The information gap closes. Good cars return to market. Buyers pay fair prices; sellers of quality cars get fair value.

Assessment
These are the best-executed graphics in the tool. The green color signals "solved" at a glance. The cost displayed in the header (and color-coded differently for each variant) creates the cost-escalation thread before the summary panel appears. The bold outcome sentence at the bottom gives students a quotable takeaway. No significant changes needed.
Minor note
Minor: On the SEC fix card, the cost "$100K–$10M+/yr" appears in red (error color), which is correct to signal "expensive." But this is a solution card — the red might subconsciously signal "this fix is bad" rather than "this fix is costly." Consider a deep orange or purple instead of error-red for the cost on this card only.

Does it illustrate the point?
Yes — this is the payoff slide for the students who are about to work on these exact topics. The Yale blue header signals importance and the topic names are immediately recognizable. Each row's connection is crisp enough to spark a "oh, that's what that is" moment.
Issue — Visually flat; all rows look equally important
Problem: Four identical beige rows with identical formatting. Students likely care more about their own topic than the others — but there's no visual hierarchy to help them locate it fast. When this appears on screen, a student working on Housing Finance has to read all four rows to find hers.
Fix: Add a tiny topic icon before each row (🏛 IPOs, 📲 Fintech, 🏠 Housing, 💳 Credit) — not for decoration, but to make rows scannable at distance. Optionally, on the projected version Rick could click each row to highlight it during discussion. But even just the icons would make this much more readable from the back of a classroom.

Assessment
Strong. Functions like a chapter indicator — students can always see where in the information story they are. The active state is clear. The sequence reads left-to-right as a natural progression.
Minor note
Minor: "Asymmetric" is economics jargon. A 16-year-old hearing this word for the first time might not connect "Asymmetric" on the bar to "sellers know more than buyers" in the narrative. A small parenthetical in the pill — e.g., "Asymmetric (seller knows)" — would anchor it without adding much clutter.
